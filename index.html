Approach

I approached this challenge as if it were my first assignment at Readlee, while imagining my supervisor is an overloaded engineer. Thus, I applied my theory of information overloads to approach the assignment. I recently submitted a paper on my theory to an academic computer-security conference, SADFE 2021. My draft is available at https://michaelgagnon.me/file/zbg-draft.pdf

Essentially, I made solid progress for 120 minutes, then spent an hour going down a fruitless rabbithole (animation), becoming stuck. So, I quickly wrapped up my work, and submitted it.

My theory, in this case, was to work independently for as long as possible, not get too overloaded by a yak shave, then submit my incompleted work. This approach benefits myself, and also my supervisor. It benefits my supervisor, because I waited to ask for help (on animation) after giving it my best effort for an hour, saving my supervisor time and energy. Perhaps my supervisor is familiar with integrating CSS animations into Vue.js, in which case a quick pointer might unblock both of us. Or perhaps my supervisor tells me to keep going down the rabbit hole, or perhaps tells me my appraoch is entirely wrong to begin with.

More generallly, supervisor-subordinate relationships, in sofware engineering, are often characterized by a delicate balance of attempting to help each other, while also avoiding unnecessary and burdensome labor. Essentially, we must balance independent work, with asking for help. If hired at Readlee, one of my top priorities will be to offload work from my supervisor (supposing they're overloaded), while als working efficiently, and coping with rabbit holes intelligently.

Animation

I believe animating trophies should be a top priority. Beautiful and pleasing animations help reinforce the nueral pathways that reward good work. I know it is possible to add wonderful animations to my system, in a minimally noninvasive manner (using transition groups from Vue.js), however I became bogged down debugging an animation system during this assignment. I am confident I am capable of figuring out the problems I encountered, it will just take time---peerhaps another hour or two, maybe more.

More generally, I would be delighted to study the scientific literature on gamification, to help us engineer rewarding reading experiences. At the very least, I am aware that frequent rewards help reinforce behavior. Thus, I have added a “1 story read” trophy, to reward students, early in their use of the app.

Code

I chose Vue.js to implement my trophy library. I believe Vue.js is a good choice for this problem, because it offers elegant MVC separation of concerns. To add the next trophy, just invoke the addNextTrophy() method. It wouldn't take much effort to add a more general addTrophy(trophyId) method, to allow arbitrary orderings of trophies.
